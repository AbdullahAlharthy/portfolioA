The Problem

Class imbalance is the real challenge

The dataset had 7,043 telecom customers — 73.5% stayed, 26.5% churned. When one class is that much larger, most models just learn to predict "no churn" and still score 73% accuracy by doing nothing useful. The challenge was to actually catch the customers who churn, not just look good on a metric that doesn't require it.

This isn't just a modeling problem. It's a problem about which metric you optimize for. Accuracy is useless here. F1-score and recall on the minority class are what matter — because a missed churner costs money, while a false alarm is just a targeted retention email.

What I Did

50+ experiments, logged systematically

I tested 10 different classifiers (Logistic Regression, Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, SVM, KNN, H2O AutoML, and others) across 6 class-balancing methods (SMOTE, SMOTETomek, SMOTEENN, BorderlineSMOTE, class weights, none) and 4 encoding schemes for categorical features.

The key addition was TabDDPM — a diffusion model for tabular data — used to generate synthetic minority-class samples. Instead of just oversampling, the model learns the underlying distribution of churners and generates new, realistic examples. This was combined with SMOTETomek for the final pipeline.

Hyperparameter tuning was done with Optuna (100 trials) on the best-performing models. All experiments used stratified 5-fold cross-validation to keep results comparable.

Key Finding

TabDDPM pushed F1 by 7%

The best pipeline was LightGBM + TabDDPM + SMOTETomek + Optuna. The final model reached F1 = 0.671 and Recall = 0.78 on the test set — meaning it correctly identified 78% of actual churners.

Adding TabDDPM-generated synthetic data moved F1 from 0.630 to 0.671 on the same LightGBM + SMOTETomek pipeline — a 7% improvement from better minority-class representation alone. This was the clearest signal that data quality matters more than model complexity at this scale.

The most predictive features were contract type, tenure, and monthly charges — consistent with domain intuition. Customers on month-to-month contracts with high monthly charges and short tenure were the most likely to churn.

Business Application

From predictions to strategy: customer segmentation

Model predictions alone aren't enough — they need to translate into actionable business decisions. By combining each customer's churn probability score with their lifetime value, every customer is classified into one of four segments, each with a distinct retention strategy.

EDA Dashboard

Exploratory analysis — understanding churn patterns

Before modeling, a full EDA dashboard was built to understand the data. Key findings: month-to-month customers churn at nearly 3× the rate of annual contracts, customers without online security churn significantly more, and the 0–12 month tenure group shows a 47.7% churn rate — dropping to 9.5% for customers who stay 49–72 months. Churned customers also pay an average of $13 more per month than retained ones, suggesting price sensitivity is a major driver.
