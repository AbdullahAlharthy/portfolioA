The Problem

VisDrone is a genuinely hard benchmark

VisDrone2019 is a notoriously difficult aerial object detection dataset. Objects are often under 10 pixels wide, hundreds of instances overlap in a single frame, and rarer classes like bicycles and tricycles get swamped by pedestrians and cars in training. Even published research papers struggle on this benchmark — state-of-the-art models often land between mAP@50 of 0.25 and 0.45.

The goal wasn't to beat the state of the art. It was to understand what actually moves the needle on a hard dataset: model architecture or training data volume?

What I Did

Progressive scaling — data first, then model

I ran four experiments in sequence, designed to isolate what matters. The first two held model architecture constant (YOLOv8n) and varied data volume and training duration. The third and fourth scaled model size while keeping data fixed at the augmented set.

The augmentation pipeline doubled the dataset from 6,471 to 12,942 images using color jitter, horizontal flips, and rotation. Rather than random augmentation, each transform was chosen to reflect realistic drone imaging conditions — varying light, heading, and orientation.

Final training used YOLO11l (190 layers, 25.3M parameters) on CUDA, evaluated on the VisDrone validation set. SHAP-style interpretation wasn't available for YOLO, so per-class mAP was used as the main analytical tool.

Key Findings

Data volume moved the needle more than architecture

Augmentation alone improved mAP@50 from 0.236 to 0.250 on the same YOLOv8n model. That's a 6% relative improvement just from data volume, before touching model size. Scaling to YOLO11l then pushed mAP@50 to 0.346 — the biggest single jump came from the model.

Interestingly, YOLOv8m and YOLO11l reached the same mAP@50. YOLO11l was selected as the final model for its superior architecture and 1.9ms real-time inference — making it more deployable if this were going to production.

Per-class results tell the clearest story about the dataset's difficulty. Cars reached 0.775 mAP — well-represented and visually distinctive from above. Bicycles stayed at 0.103 — underrepresented in training and visually similar to motorcycles and pedestrians at low resolution. This is a data problem, not a model problem.
