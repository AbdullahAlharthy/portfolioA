The Problem

Five datasets, one messy reality

Five public clinical datasets existed for heart disease — each with different formats, different column names, different scales, and missing values throughout. The UCI Cleveland dataset is the famous one, but using it alone means leaving out thousands of additional patient records. The first challenge was getting them into a single, clean, usable format.

Once merged, the question was: which decision tree setup generalizes best, and do natural patient subgroups exist in the data that a supervised model might miss?

What I Did

Systematic DT comparison + unsupervised clustering

After cleaning and merging all 5 datasets into 1,889 records with 14 clinical features, I used RapidMiner to run a structured comparison of decision tree configurations. I varied two things: the splitting criterion (Information Gain, Gini Index, Information Gain Ratio) and the minimum leaf size (3.5%, 4.5%, 5.0%, 6.0% of training data). That's 12 configurations total, all evaluated on the same held-out test set.

For clustering, I applied K-Means with K ranging from 2 to 5, using the Elbow Method on within-cluster sum of squares to choose K. The goal was to see if the data naturally separated into clinically meaningful patient groups without using the diagnosis label at all.

Key Findings

Smaller leaf sizes consistently win

The best configuration was Information Gain with 3.5% minimum leaf size — achieving 85% accuracy and an overall score of 0.87. The pattern held across all three splitting criteria: smaller leaf sizes (more specific splits) outperformed larger ones, suggesting the clinical decision boundaries are genuinely granular and benefit from more refined splits.

K-Means selected K=3 via the Elbow Method, revealing three distinct patient clusters. The key separating features were thalassemia type, chest pain category (atypical angina specifically), and ST segment slope — consistent with clinical literature on cardiac risk stratification. This was an unsupervised confirmation of features that the supervised model also weighted heavily.

Cluster 0 (42%) represented younger patients with iron-type thalassemia and lower risk profiles. Cluster 1 (3%) was a small group of outliers. Cluster 2 (55%) was the high-risk group, predominantly characterized by ST slope abnormalities.
